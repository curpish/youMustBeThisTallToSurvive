Operation Manual — Supplementary Bulletin (v2)

Revision Note
This bulletin supplements Operation Manual v1 following the Control Panel
retrofit. It does not replace v1. Where the two disagree, follow whichever
one your supervisor read most recently. Two additional Electron Crawler
Lumination Indicators ("T" and "W"... no — "T" and "Y") have been installed
since v1 was printed, and the Basket Launch subsystem has been cleared for
operator-assisted use. This bulletin explains both. Read it before your
next shift. Do not read it during your next shift. There is no good time
to read it during your shift.

Understanding the Control Panel Buttons (Revised Count: Six)

The Control Panel Buttons are now designated Q, W, E, R, T, and Y. As
established in v1, these are Electron Crawler Lumination Indicators as well
as Fault Correction Hydrographic Controllers — they light up when a fault
is found, and they clear the fault when pressed correctly. "Correctly" is
the operative word, and it is new information not covered in v1.

The Mode Select dial does more than v1 lets on. Each lit fault has a
required Mode (1, 2, or 3, color-coded green / amber / red on the
indicator itself) and the dial must be turned to match that Mode BEFORE
the corresponding button is pressed. Pressing a button while the dial is
on the wrong Mode does nothing. The fault will remain lit. The fault will
not be impressed by your effort.

Correction Procedure:
1. Watch the six indicators. When one lights, note its color.
2. Turn the Mode Select dial to the matching position (green = 1,
   amber = 2, red = 3).
3. Press the lit button. It will go dark. You may feel a small, hollow
   sense of accomplishment. This is normal and not logged anywhere.

Faults appear more often the faster the wheel turns, and more often still
the longer your shift runs — the Hydrographic Controllers wear down
across a shift the same way everything else on this machine does.
Corporate has elected not to call this "fatigue" in writing.

WARNING: If all six indicators are lit simultaneously, the Control Panel
will execute an unscheduled, fully automatic Big Stop with no input from
the operator. This is not a feature. It is what happens when nobody does
anything for long enough. The panel will reset itself to all-green
afterward, and the day will continue as though it hadn't happened, which
it will not have, on paper.

Understanding Basket Launch Procedure (New)

This section did not exist in v1 because the Basket Launch subsystem was
not yet cleared for operator-assisted use. It is now. Congratulations.

The Throttle Lever moves the wheel through five bands: Static, Jog,
Nominal, Exceedance, and Overspeed. Under normal conditions the Governor
will not allow the wheel past the Nominal band. This is a safety feature
and you are about to be asked to defeat it.

To exceed the governed limit, retrieve the Governor Bypass Screwdriver
from its mount and engage it at the governor housing. The Bypass does not
take effect immediately — it needs a moment to seat before it lets go.
Once it releases, you have a limited window of full authority over wheel
speed before the Governor reasserts itself and the system needs to
recover before it can be bypassed again. Plan your Launch attempt inside
that window, not after it.

As the wheel approaches Overspeed, watch the Axle Heat Gauge. A narrow
band of it will begin to glow — this is the Basket Release Tolerance, and
it is the only acceptable place to pull the Big Stop. Pulling Big Stop
while the needle sits inside the glow performs a Successful Launch.
Pulling it outside the glow, or letting the wheel reach true Overspeed
without pulling it at all, performs an Unsuccessful Launch — the machine
will perform an automatic Big Stop on your behalf the moment it hits the
limit, and it will count against you exactly the same as if you had
pulled it yourself at the wrong moment.

Pulling Big Stop — whether you land inside the glow or not — narrows the
Tolerance band for the rest of the shift. It does not return to its
original width. The machine remembers every Big Stop you have ever
performed and likes you slightly less each time. Plan accordingly: your
first few Launches of the day are the most forgiving you will ever get.

Understanding Axle Wear and the Last Chance Protocol (New)

Running the wheel above Nominal speed wears the axle whether or not a
Launch is successful. This wear does not go away when the wheel slows
back down — corporate refers to this as the Cumulative Axle Fatigue
Index; everyone else calls it "the floor," because the gauge's resting
point quietly creeps upward and never creeps back. Run hot often enough
and the floor will eventually sit so high that the machine enters Last
Chance status.

WARNING: Once the machine has entered Last Chance status, any subsequent
hot run that ends in an Unsuccessful Launch — or no Launch at all — will
result in Catastrophic Axle Failure. The wheel separates from its
supports and departs the platform under its own power. This ends your
shift. It is, in every sense that matters to you personally, the worst
possible outcome, ranked only slightly below several outcomes the basket
occupants are contractually presumed to have accepted.

Shift Completion Criteria

A shift is considered successfully completed after six Successful
Launches. The wheel will perform a celebratory maneuver outside its
normal operating parameters; this is sanctioned, logged, and the one time
all day the Governor is allowed to lose the argument on purpose.

Understanding Service Tier Selection

Before clocking in, you will be asked to select a Service Tier: Normal or
Hard. Normal widens the Basket Release Tolerance and slows the rate at
which Control Panel Buttons light up. Hard does neither of those things.
Corporate has not clarified why Hard exists as an option, only that it
does, and that selecting it was your choice.

Maintenance Addendum
Maintenance assures operators that, with proper daily upkeep, the
Tolerance band will never narrow unexpectedly, the Hydrographic
Controllers will never light up faster than an operator can reasonably
respond to, and the Cumulative Axle Fatigue Index will never reach Last
Chance status during a properly staffed shift. Maintenance has not
updated this assurance since v1 and does not intend to.
